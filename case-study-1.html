Redesigning the lending workflow for a Series B fintech

An end-to-end redesign of a loan origination platform — from discovery to handoff — that cut processing time by 40% and lifted application completion from 52% to 81%.

Result

01 · Context

The problem: a 9-step form nobody finished

The client — a Series B NBFC — had a digital loan origination flow that had grown organically over two years. What started as a 4-step form had ballooned to 9 steps, each owned by a different team. The result: a 52% completion rate, and a median processing time of 6.5 days per application.

I was brought in as the lead designer to audit the existing product, define the redesign scope, and own the UX end-to-end — from research to final handoff with annotated specs.

02 · Discovery

What the data and users told us

I ran a 3-week discovery sprint. This included session recordings from Hotjar across 1,200 sessions, 8 user interviews with both borrowers and internal loan officers, and a stakeholder alignment workshop with the product and ops teams.

03 · Process

How I structured the work

1

Journey mapping & problem taxonomy

Mapped the full borrower journey from awareness to disbursement. Identified 14 distinct friction points and grouped them into 4 categories: information overload, poor error feedback, broken progress logic, and unclear status communication.

2

Prioritisation with the PM

Used a RICE framework to stack-rank each fix. The top 6 items covered 82% of the projected impact. Agreed to descope the "loan advisor" chat feature for v1 — it added complexity without addressing core completion rate issues.

3

Low-fi wireframes & concept testing

Wireframed 3 structural approaches for collapsing the 9 steps. Ran unmoderated tests on Maze with 28 participants. Concept C — a linear-with-sidebar-progress layout — had a 94% task completion rate vs 61% for the accordion approach.

4

High-fidelity design & component library

Built a full component library in Figma — 48 components across form elements, status states, upload flows, and notification patterns. Every component was documented with usage rules and accessibility notes.

5

Handoff, QA & iteration

Delivered annotated specs in Figma with interaction notes for every state. Did 3 rounds of design QA with the engineering team before each sprint release. Tracked 11 edge-case bugs through Jira and resolved all before go-live.

04 · Key design decisions

Three decisions that drove the outcome

A

Collapsing 9 steps into 4 logical stages

Restructured the flow into: Identity, Financials, Documents, and Review. Each stage had a clear completion signal and saved progress automatically every 30 seconds. This alone accounted for a 22% lift in completion at the document step.

B

Inline document upload with real-time validation

Replaced the multi-page document step with an inline upload component that validated file type, size, and resolution instantly. Added a "mobile handoff" option — borrowers could SMS a link to themselves to upload from their phone camera.

C

Unified loan officer dashboard with application queue

Built a parallel internal dashboard for loan officers. All borrower-submitted data flowed directly into a structured review queue — eliminating the manual re-entry that was causing 1–2 day delays per application. Status updates synced back to the borrower in real time.

06 · Learnings

What I'd do differently

The biggest miss was not involving loan officers in the early discovery phase. I had strong borrower insight but had to backtrack two weeks when we found that the officer-side queue design conflicted with how approvals were routed internally. Getting ops involved in week 1, not week 6, would have saved us a full sprint.

I also underestimated the mobile handoff feature — it became one of the most-used features and wasn't in the original scope. It came from a single user interview comment. That reinforced for me: don't dismiss offhand remarks. They often surface the real workaround.
