01 · Context

No dedicated tool — just experience and scattered PDFs

RWE operates one of the world’s largest offshore wind fleets. When a turbine faults, maintenance teams need to diagnose the issue, find root causes, and create work orders — often under time pressure and harsh offshore conditions.

The problem: there was no dedicated troubleshooting tool. Teams relied on SAP transaction codes, scattered PDF manuals, and the tribal knowledge of experienced technicians. Information was siloed, hard to find, and impossible to standardize across sites.

I was brought in as the lead product designer to work alongside BCG consultants and internal stakeholders to design a new digital tool from scratch — covering the entire troubleshooting and documentation workflow.

04 · Problem framing

Four fault scenarios, two product modules

From the research, we identified four distinct fault scenarios that technicians encounter. Each requires a different lookup and resolution path, which informed the product’s information architecture.

Known fault — own site

The fault has occurred before at this wind farm. The resolution is documented locally — the challenge is finding it quickly.

Known fault — other site

Another RWE wind farm has solved this exact fault. But knowledge doesn’t travel between sites — it sits in someone’s head or a local PDF.

Known fault — multiple sites

The fault has been resolved at multiple sites. Consolidating root causes and comparing approaches would save significant diagnosis time.

Unknown fault

A completely new fault with no prior resolution. Requires on-site investigation, hypothesis testing, and thorough documentation for future reference.

Product scope

Module 1: Troubleshooting

Fault search, root cause lookup across sites, consolidated cause comparison, and work order drafting. Primarily used by the Maintenance Coordinator on desktop.

Module 2: Documentation

Structured capture of repair outcomes, photos, notes, and knowledge transfer. Primarily used by Technicians on iPad while offshore or immediately post-trip.

05 · Process

How I structured the work

1

Research & shadowing

Conducted interviews and contextual shadowing sessions with ~10 participants across maintenance coordinators, lead technicians, and offshore technicians. Observed real troubleshooting workflows in control rooms and reviewed existing documentation practices.

2

As-is journey mapping

Mapped the end-to-end troubleshooting journey across both user roles, covering every location transition — SCADA alarm in the control room, offshore travel, turbine-level work, and post-trip documentation. Identified critical pain points and information gaps at each stage.

3

Vision journey & information architecture

Defined the target-state journey — where the new tool would intervene. Designed the IA around two modules (Troubleshooting + Documentation) and four fault scenarios. Aligned with BCG consultants, Team Leads, and Product Owners on scope and priorities.

4

Wireframing & prototyping

Designed key screens for both modules in Figma: dashboard with fault search, fault results, consolidated root causes, work order drafts, and the technician documentation flow. Iterated rapidly with BCG and internal stakeholders.

5

Steering & handoff

As lead designer, I steered an external BCG designer while maintaining ownership of all design decisions. Facilitated reviews with Business Partners and Product Owners. Delivered a production-ready prototype with full interaction specs.

08 · Key design decisions

Three decisions that shaped the product

A

Cross-site fault search as the entry point

The biggest unlock was making fault resolutions searchable across all RWE wind farms. Previously, a technician at Site A had no way to know that Site B had already solved the exact same fault. The search-first IA means the most common action (looking up a known fault) is also the fastest.

B

Consolidated root cause comparison

When a fault has been resolved at multiple sites, the tool shows all root causes side-by-side with frequency data. This lets coordinators pick the most likely cause without calling colleagues at other sites — eliminating one of the biggest time sinks in the as-is workflow.

C

Integrated work order → documentation loop

Work orders created in Module 1 flow directly into Module 2 as structured documentation templates on the technician’s iPad. This closes the feedback loop: the diagnosis informs the task, and the task outcome feeds back into the knowledge base for future troubleshooting.

10 · Learnings

What I took from this project

Steering ≠ stepping back. Managing an external BCG designer taught me that steering someone else’s output requires even more clarity than doing the work yourself. Every design decision needed to be articulated as a principle, not just a preference — otherwise the work drifted between review cycles.

Shadowing > interviews for industrial tools. The most valuable insights came from watching technicians work, not from asking them questions. In interviews, they described the “official” process. In shadowing, I saw the workarounds — the sticky notes on monitors, the WhatsApp groups for fault advice, the informal knowledge networks that actually kept things running.

Cross-site knowledge is the real product. The tool’s biggest value isn’t the UI — it’s making one site’s experience available to every other site. The design needed to make contribution feel effortless (Module 2) so the database (Module 1) would actually grow over time. The documentation flow is the flywheel, not a secondary feature.
